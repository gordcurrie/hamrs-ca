# B-001-012: Third-Party Messages and Confidentiality

## Payment Prohibition

Amateur radio operators may **never** accept payment of any kind for exchanging messages. This includes direct payment, gifts, favours, or any form of remuneration. Third-party message handling is a public service — the moment money changes hands, it becomes a commercial service, which requires different licensing entirely.

## Divulging Radio Communications

Canadian law distinguishes between different categories of radio communication with respect to confidentiality:

- **Broadcasting** (commercial broadcasts): the content is intended for the public and may be divulged freely
- **Amateur radio transmissions**: also may be divulged — amateur communications are not private in the same way that point-to-point communications are

However, it is an offence to divulge the content of a non-broadcasting transmission **for the purpose of answering questions from a media organization**. The prohibition is on commercial exploitation of intercepted communications, not on casual discussion.

## Third-Party Traffic

Third parties are persons who are not amateur radio operators and who are communicating via an amateur station. In Canada, third-party communications:
- Must be of a **personal and non-commercial nature**
- Canada does not prohibit international third-party communications (unlike some countries)
- Amateur stations may provide third-party communications with **any other amateur radio station**

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Payment for handling messages: **never permitted**
- Amateur radio transmissions **may** be divulged
- Offence: divulging content to a media organization for their purposes
- Third-party messages must be **personal and non-commercial**
- Canada allows **international** third-party communications

# B-004-003: Bipolar Transistors

## What a Transistor Does

A **bipolar junction transistor (BJT)** is a three-terminal semiconductor device that can **amplify** or **switch** signals. It is called "bipolar" because both electrons and holes carry current inside it.

## Terminals

- **Base (B)** — the control terminal; small input current here
- **Collector (C)** — current flows in here (in NPN)
- **Emitter (E)** — current flows out here (in NPN)

## NPN vs PNP

Two types exist:
- **NPN**: current flows from collector to emitter when base is driven positive relative to emitter. Most common in circuits.
- **PNP**: current flows from emitter to collector when base is driven negative relative to emitter (complement of NPN).

## How Amplification Works

A small **base current** controls a much larger **collector current**. The ratio is called **beta (β)** or **hFE** — the current gain. A transistor with β = 100 lets 1 mA of base current control 100 mA of collector current. The output power comes from the DC supply, not the input — the transistor just controls the valve.

## Transistor as a Switch

Driven hard into **saturation**, a transistor acts like a closed switch (collector-emitter nearly shorted). With no base current (**cutoff**), it acts like an open switch. This is how transistors work in digital logic and relay replacement circuits.

## Amplifier Classes

The operating point (bias) determines which **class** the amplifier runs:
- **Class A**: transistor conducts for the full 360° of the input cycle — linear, low distortion, low efficiency (~25–30%)
- **Class B**: conducts for 180° — higher efficiency (~78%), but crossover distortion without careful biasing
- **Class C**: conducts for less than 180° — highest efficiency (~80%+), used in RF power amplifiers, not suitable for SSB (non-linear)

## Exam-Critical Facts

- BJT terminals: **base, collector, emitter**
- NPN: small **base current** controls large **collector current**
- Current gain (beta, β): ratio of **collector current to base current**
- Saturation = transistor acts as **closed switch**
- Cutoff = transistor acts as **open switch**
- Class A: conducts **360°** — most linear, least efficient
- Class B: conducts **180°**
- Class C: conducts **less than 180°** — most efficient, used in RF PA stages

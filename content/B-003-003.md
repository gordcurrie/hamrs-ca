# B-003-003: Superheterodyne Receiver and FM Receiver Stages

## The Superheterodyne Principle

The superheterodyne (superhet) receiver is the dominant receiver architecture for virtually all modern radio equipment. The core idea: convert the received signal to a fixed **intermediate frequency (IF)** using a local oscillator and mixer, then do most of the amplification and filtering at that fixed frequency. This allows the IF filter to be optimized once for narrow bandwidth and high selectivity, regardless of which operating frequency is selected.

## Stage-by-Stage

**RF amplifier (front-end)**: The first active stage after the antenna. Amplifies the incoming signal before mixing. On VHF, this stage must produce **very little noise** — noise generated here gets amplified along with the signal through the entire receiver chain. This is why low-noise amplifiers (LNAs) are critical on VHF/UHF.

**Mixer**: Combines the received RF signal with the local oscillator signal to produce the IF. This is the frequency conversion stage. Regardless of which frequency you tune to, the mixer outputs the same IF — **the mixer allows detection to function at a single frequency regardless of received frequency**.

**Local oscillator**: Sets the received frequency. When you turn the tuning dial, you are adjusting the local oscillator frequency. The IF = RF ± LO (depending on design). **The local oscillator sets the received frequency**.

**IF filter**: A fixed filter centred on the IF frequency. This stage **rejects signals on adjacent channels** — its bandwidth determines the receiver's selectivity. Narrow = more selective, less bandwidth; wide = more audio but more interference.

**IF amplifier**: Provides most of the signal gain in the receiver. Works at the fixed IF frequency where filtering is already applied, so it only amplifies the desired signal.

**Detector/discriminator (FM)**: Recovers the original audio from the carrier. For FM, this is called a discriminator.

**Limiter (FM only)**: Removes amplitude variations from the FM signal before the discriminator. FM carries information only in frequency — any amplitude variation is noise or interference. The limiter clips the signal to a constant amplitude, improving noise rejection. The loud noise heard from an FM receiver in the absence of a signal is due to the **very large gain of stages ahead of the discriminator** — without a valid signal, noise is amplified to full output.

**AF amplifier**: The final stage. The volume control adjusts the AF amplifier. This stage also typically includes the **squelch circuit** in FM receivers — squelch mutes the AF amplifier in the absence of a sufficient signal.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- RF amplifier ("front-end"): must produce **very little noise** on VHF
- Mixer: allows detection at **a single frequency regardless of received frequency**
- Local oscillator: **sets the received frequency**
- IF filter: **rejects signals on adjacent channels** (selectivity)
- IF amplifier: provides **most of the receiver gain**
- FM limiter: **removes amplitude variations** from received signal
- FM discriminator: **recovers the original modulation** from the carrier
- Volume control controls: **AF amplifier**
- Squelch circuit is in: **AF amplifier**

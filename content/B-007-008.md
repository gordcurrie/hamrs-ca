# B-007-008: Propagation Prediction and Practical Tips

## Using Propagation Forecasts

Propagation conditions change daily with solar activity and time of day. Tools for predicting conditions:

- **Solar flux index (SFI)**: published daily; higher = better HF (especially higher bands). Optimal SFI is 150+.
- **K index**: geomagnetic activity; lower is better. K = 0–2 is quiet; K ≥ 5 is storm conditions — avoid polar paths.
- **A index**: daily geomagnetic activity summary; A < 15 is generally good.
- **DX clusters and spotting networks**: real-time reports from stations who are actually making contacts; most reliable way to know what is actually open right now.
- **WWV / WWVH**: time signal stations that broadcast solar flux and K-index bulletins at 18 minutes past each hour (WWV at 2.5, 5, 10, 15, 20 MHz).

## Beacon Networks

**Propagation beacons** transmit continuously on known frequencies. If you can hear a beacon, you know that propagation path is open. The **NCDXF/IARU International Beacon Project** operates 18 beacons worldwide, each cycling through 14, 18, 21, 24.9, and 28 MHz. Monitoring 10 m (28 MHz) beacons is the fastest way to detect high-band openings.

## Grey-Line Propagation

The **grey line** is the terminator — the boundary between day and night on Earth's surface. D-layer absorption is minimal along the grey line (the D layer has not yet formed or is just fading), while the F layer is still ionized. This creates a brief window each day (around local sunrise and sunset) when long-distance paths on the lower HF bands (40 m, 80 m) are enhanced.

## Choosing the Right Band

A practical decision guide:
1. Check the solar flux and K index
2. Note the time of day (day = lower bands; night = 20/40/80 m)
3. Estimate the distance to target (short = higher frequency; long distance = lower to mid HF)
4. Try listening first — if the band sounds quiet and you hear DX stations, try calling

## CW vs. SSB Propagation

**CW (Morse code)** occupies less bandwidth and can be copied reliably through noise and selective fading conditions that make SSB unintelligible. For marginal propagation paths, CW provides several dB of effective advantage. Digital modes (FT8, JS8) extend this further — FT8 can be decoded 10–15 dB below the noise floor.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Solar flux: higher = **better HF propagation on higher bands**
- K index: lower = **quieter geomagnetic conditions** (better HF)
- K ≥ 5: **geomagnetic storm** — HF via polar paths is disrupted
- WWV/WWVH broadcasts: solar flux and K-index at **18 minutes past each hour**
- Grey-line: enhanced propagation along the **day/night terminator**
- Propagation beacons: used to detect **when a band is open**
- CW advantage over SSB: **narrower bandwidth** — works in weaker signal conditions

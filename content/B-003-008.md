# B-003-008: Linear Power Supplies

## What a Linear Power Supply Does

A linear power supply converts AC mains voltage (120V or 240V) to a regulated DC output voltage (e.g., 13.8V for a 12V transceiver). "Linear" refers to the fact that the voltage regulator operates in its linear region — continuously dissipating the voltage difference between input and output as heat, rather than switching on and off.

## Stage-by-Stage

**Transformer**: Converts the AC mains voltage **up or down** to the required level. Also provides **galvanic isolation** between the mains and the output — the secondary winding is not electrically connected to the primary, protecting the user and equipment from direct connection to the mains.

**Rectifier**: Converts **alternating current into direct current**. Typically a bridge rectifier using four diodes. The output is pulsating DC — it's DC (one polarity) but it still varies at twice the mains frequency (120 Hz in North America).

**Filter (smoothing capacitor)**: Smooths out the **pulsating direct current** to a more stable DC level. Large electrolytic capacitors charge during each rectifier pulse and discharge between pulses, reducing the ripple voltage.

**Regulator**: Ensures that the output voltage **stays constant when the load varies**. As the current demand changes (e.g., transmitter transmitting vs. receiving), the regulator compensates to maintain the set voltage. The regulator is the stage that dissipates the most heat — it requires a **heat sink**.

**Overvoltage protection** (if present): Monitors voltage **at the output of the regulator**. If the output exceeds the set limit (e.g., due to regulator failure), the protection circuit shuts down or crowbars the output to protect the load.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Transformer purpose: **convert AC mains voltage** and provide **isolation**
- Rectifier purpose: **convert alternating current into direct current**
- Filter purpose: **smooth out pulsating direct current**
- Regulator purpose: **ensure voltage stays constant** when load varies
- Regulator typically requires: **a heat sink**
- Overvoltage protection monitors: **output of the regulator**

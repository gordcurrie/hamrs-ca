# B-005-006: AC Fundamentals — Frequency, Wavelength, and RMS

## What is AC?

**Alternating current (AC)** reverses direction periodically. A **sine wave** is the fundamental AC waveform — voltage and current rise to a positive peak, return through zero, go to a negative peak, and repeat.

## Frequency and Period

**Frequency (f)** — the number of complete cycles per second, measured in **hertz (Hz)**.

**Period (T)** — the time for one complete cycle:

> **T = 1 / f**    and    **f = 1 / T**

Example: 60 Hz mains → T = 1/60 ≈ **16.7 milliseconds**
Example: 14 MHz → T = 1/14,000,000 ≈ **71 nanoseconds**

## Wavelength

**Wavelength (λ)** — the physical distance a wave travels in one cycle. In free space, radio waves travel at the speed of light (c ≈ 300,000,000 m/s):

> **λ = c / f** or approximately **λ (metres) = 300 / f (MHz)**

Examples:
- 14 MHz → λ = 300/14 ≈ **21.4 m** (20 metre band)
- 146 MHz → λ = 300/146 ≈ **2.05 m** (2 metre band)
- 440 MHz → λ = 300/440 ≈ **0.68 m = 68 cm** (70 cm band)

## RMS Voltage

A sine wave's **peak voltage** is the maximum value. The **RMS (root-mean-square)** voltage is the DC-equivalent heating value — the DC voltage that delivers the same power to a resistor.

> **V_RMS = V_peak × 0.707** (or V_peak / √2)

> **V_peak = V_RMS × 1.414** (or V_RMS × √2)

**Household mains (Canada)**: 120 V RMS → peak = 120 × 1.414 ≈ **170 V peak**

Voltmeters read RMS for AC. Power calculations use RMS voltages and currents.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Frequency unit: **hertz (Hz)**
- Period: T = **1 / f**
- Wavelength formula: **λ = 300 / f(MHz)** (metres)
- 7 MHz wavelength: 300/7 ≈ **42.8 m** (40 metre band)
- 144 MHz wavelength: 300/144 ≈ **2.08 m**
- RMS = peak × **0.707**
- Peak = RMS × **1.414**
- Canadian mains: **120 V RMS**, approximately **170 V peak**
- AC voltmeter reads: **RMS**

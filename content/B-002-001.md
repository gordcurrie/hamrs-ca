# B-002-001: Repeater Operation

## What a Repeater Does

A repeater's primary purpose is to **increase the range of portable and mobile stations**. A hand-held radio with 5 watts and a rubber duck antenna can talk to someone 50 km away through a repeater on a hilltop — something impossible on simplex.

The repeater receives on one frequency (the input) and simultaneously retransmits on another (the output). The difference between input and output frequencies is the **offset**. Standard 2-metre offset: **600 kHz**.

## Making Contact on a Repeater

To call a specific station:
- Say **the other station's call sign**, then **your call sign**

To join a conversation in progress:
- Wait for a pause between transmissions, then **say your call sign**

## Good Operating Practice

**Pause before and between transmissions**: This allows other stations to break in, especially for emergencies. Someone with an urgent message needs to get through.

**Keep transmissions short**: A long unbroken transmission prevents someone with an emergency from accessing the repeater.

**Repeater time-out timer**: Most repeaters have a timer (typically 3–5 minutes) that interrupts excessively long transmissions. This prevents one station from monopolizing the repeater and protects the repeater's duty cycle.

## CTCSS Tones

**CTCSS (Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System)** is a sub-audible tone transmitted with your audio. The repeater's receiver is configured to open only when it detects the correct tone — this prevents the repeater from activating on noise or on other repeaters that happen to be on the same frequency. You hear no difference in audio; the tone is below the normal audio range (~67–254 Hz).

## Frequency Coordination

On VHF and UHF bands, **frequency coordination** is the process of recommending frequencies to minimize interference between neighbouring repeaters. It is not a regulatory requirement per se — it is a voluntary cooperative process administered by regional frequency coordinators (in Canada, often through the Radio Amateurs of Canada or regional groups).

## Location Questions

The accepted way to ask someone's location on a repeater: **"Where are you?"** — plain language is preferred over jargon or Q codes in voice operation.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Repeater purpose: **increase range of portable and mobile stations**
- Calling a specific station: say **their call sign, then yours**
- Joining a conversation: say **your call sign** during a break
- Pause before/between transmissions: to allow **emergency break-ins**
- Time-out timer: **interrupts lengthy transmissions**
- CTCSS: **sub-audible tone** that activates receiver audio when present
- 2-metre standard offset: **600 kHz**
- Frequency coordination: **voluntary process** to minimize interference between repeaters

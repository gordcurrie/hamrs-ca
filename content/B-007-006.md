# B-007-006: HF Band Characteristics

## How Different HF Bands Behave

Each amateur HF band has characteristic propagation behaviour determined by frequency, time of day, season, and solar cycle. Knowing which band to use when is a core practical skill.

## 160 Metres (1.8–2.0 MHz)

- **Ground wave**: extends to 50–100 km
- **Sky wave**: works at night only — D layer absorbs heavily during daylight
- **DX**: possible winter nights when path is entirely in darkness
- Character: heavy noise, atmospheric static, demanding; called "Top Band"

## 80 Metres (3.5–4.0 MHz)

- **Day**: primarily ground wave and regional sky wave; D-layer absorption limits DX
- **Night**: D layer disappears; 80 m opens for continental and intercontinental sky wave
- Popular for local and regional nets (ARES, traffic nets)

## 40 Metres (7.0–7.3 MHz)

- **Day**: regional; some DX in summer
- **Night**: excellent DX, especially winter; sky-wave range 2000–6000 km
- The most reliable DX band for all conditions; international broadcast interference can be heavy

## 20 Metres (14.0–14.35 MHz)

- **The workhorse DX band** — open nearly 24 hours during solar maximum
- Reliable intercontinental paths
- During solar minimum, may close at night; daytime and afternoon are the best times

## 15 Metres (21.0–21.45 MHz)

- Good DX during solar maximum; often dead during solar minimum
- Best propagation: midday; closes at night

## 10 Metres (28.0–29.7 MHz)

- **Spectacular DX during solar maximum** — even low power can span continents
- Nearly dead during solar minimum
- Sporadic-E openings can occur even at minimum; supports local simplex FM at top of band

## General Rules

- Higher frequencies require **higher MUF** (more solar activity)
- Lower bands: more reliable year-round, more noise, shorter range per hop
- **Grey-line** (the sunrise/sunset terminator): D layer is thin, F layer still active — brief periods of excellent long-path DX on 40 m, 80 m

## Exam-Critical Facts

- 80/40 m daytime: **D-layer absorption** limits sky wave; ground wave and regional use
- 80/40 m night: **D layer gone** → long-distance sky wave opens
- 20 m: most reliable **DX band**, usable nearly 24 hours at solar maximum
- 10 m: excellent DX at **solar maximum**; nearly dead at **solar minimum**
- Grey-line: brief window of excellent DX as **terminator** passes between stations
- Noise/static: worst on **lower HF bands** (160–80 m)

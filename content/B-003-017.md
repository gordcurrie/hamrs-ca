# B-003-017: Power Supplies in Practice

## What a Power Supply Does

A power supply converts one form of electrical power to another — typically 120V AC mains to 13.8V DC for a transceiver. **A device that converts 120V AC to 12V DC is a power supply**.

## Selecting a Power Supply

When selecting a 13.8V DC power supply for a transceiver, the most important specification is **output current capability**. A 100W HF transceiver at full transmit can draw 20+ amperes. The supply must deliver the peak current without excessive voltage drop.

## Linear vs. Switching Power Supplies

**Linear power supply**:
- Advantages: **lower risk of radio frequency noise** — the linear regulator doesn't switch, so it doesn't generate switching frequencies that can land on amateur bands
- Disadvantages: less efficient, heavier (large transformer and filter capacitors)

**Switching (switch-mode) power supply (SMPS)**:
- Advantages: **reduced physical dimensions and weight**, more efficient
- Disadvantages: switching frequencies can produce RFI that appears as noise across multiple amateur bands

For an amateur station, a linear supply is often preferred specifically because it produces less radio noise.

## Hum on Transmission

A loud, low-frequency hum on your transmission is typically a **power supply problem** — a failing filter capacitor that is not adequately smoothing the 120 Hz ripple from the rectifier. First place to look: the power supply filter.

## DC Wiring for Mobile Installations

**Fuse location**: In a mobile installation, the fuse in the DC line must be **as near to the battery as possible**. This protects the entire circuit — the wiring between the battery and the fuse would be unprotected if the fuse were at the radio end.

**Wire gauge**: Heavy-gauge wire is used for a 100W transceiver's DC power connection to **minimize the voltage drop**. A thin wire has significant resistance; under 20A load, even a small resistance causes noticeable voltage drop (V = IR).

To calculate required gauge: voltage drop limit ÷ (current × distance factor) → pick the gauge with lowest acceptable loss per metre at required current.

**Why fuse the positive lead**: To prevent an overcurrent situation (e.g., a shorted lead rubbing against chassis metal) from **starting a fire**. Vehicle wiring shorts are a real fire hazard.

## Simple Supply + CW Transmitter

A simple DC power supply using a transformer, rectifier, and filter capacitor (no regulator) will have voltage ripple that varies with transmit current draw. Powering a CW transmitter from this causes **chirp** — the voltage sag each time the key is pressed shifts the oscillator frequency.

## Mains Voltages

Standard North American home mains voltages: **120 volts and 240 volts** (for high-current loads like dryers, ranges).

# B-001-019: Technical Standards — Frequency and Modulation

## Frequency Stability

The regulations require that all amateur stations have **frequency stability equivalent to crystal control** on frequencies **below 148 MHz**. This does not mean you must use a crystal — modern synthesizers and PLLs easily meet this standard — but your transmitter must not drift significantly. Frequency accuracy protects adjacent channels and makes nets work reliably.

All stations must also be capable of **reliably measuring their operating frequency**, regardless of emission mode. You cannot transmit without knowing what frequency you are on.

## Overmodulation

SSB transmitters (and voice modes generally) must have **means to prevent or indicate overmodulation**. Overmodulation causes your signal to spread beyond its authorized bandwidth, potentially causing splatter that interferes with adjacent frequencies.

The specific regulatory requirement applies to **SSB** — the Regulations call out SSB as the emission mode requiring overmodulation protection or indication.

The maximum permitted modulation is **100%** for voice communications. Exceeding 100% causes distortion and bandwidth expansion.

## Why These Rules Exist

Unstable or poorly calibrated transmitters create problems for everyone in a band. A drifting signal is hard to follow and may wander onto someone else's frequency. Overmodulated SSB "splatters" across adjacent channels, effectively jamming nearby stations. These technical standards maintain order in a shared spectrum.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Frequency stability: must be equivalent to **crystal control** on frequencies **below 148 MHz**
- All stations must be able to **reliably measure their operating frequency**
- Emission mode requiring overmodulation protection: **SSB**
- Maximum modulation permitted for voice: **100%**
- Overmodulation indication/prevention: required equipment, not just good practice

# B-003-020: Antenna Safety — Lightning and Physical Hazards

## Lightning Protection

**Best protection from lightning damage**: **Disconnect all equipment from the power lines and antenna cables** when the station is not in use. No surge protector or lightning arrestor is as effective as a physical disconnect.

When you ground antenna and rotator cables at the station when not in use, you **help protect the station equipment and building from lightning damage** — a direct strike on the antenna can send enormous currents down the feedline. A ground path at the entry point diverts this current before it reaches the equipment.

**Lightning surge protector placement**: Install it **outside, as close to earth grounding as possible** — at the entry point where the feedline enters the building. This is where the current needs to be diverted to ground before entering.

## Tower and Antenna Work

**Fall arrest equipment**: Anyone working on an antenna tower must wear **approved fall arrest equipment** (safety harness connected to the tower). A fall from even a modest height is potentially fatal.

**Hard hat requirement for ground crew**: If you are on the ground assisting someone working on a tower, wear a **hard hat** to protect against **something dropped from the tower** (tools, hardware, antenna elements).

**Antenna height above ground**: A horizontal wire antenna should be **high enough so that no one can touch any part of it from the ground** — RF-energized antennas can cause RF burns. Similarly, open-wire feedlines must be out of reach because **high-voltage RF energy might burn the person**.

**Ground-mounted verticals**: Keep people at a **safe distance** from the antenna base. Ground-mounted antennas have high field strength near the base and can cause RF exposure issues.

## Before Antenna Repairs

Safety precautions before beginning repairs on an antenna:
- **Turn off the transmitter**
- **Disconnect the transmission line**

Both steps are required. Turning off the transmitter while the line is still connected could allow RF to be present if someone accidentally keys the radio. Disconnecting the feedline ensures no RF can reach the antenna even if the transmitter is accidentally activated.

## UHF and Microwave Safety

At UHF and microwave frequencies, RF energy can cause significant tissue heating, particularly in the eyes (which lack the blood flow to dissipate heat effectively). Keep antennas away from your eyes when RF is applied. See B-003-021 for more on RF safety at these frequencies.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Best lightning protection: **disconnect from power and antenna cables** when not in use
- Grounding cables when station is off: protects from **lightning damage**
- Surge protector placement: **outside, as close to earth grounding as possible**
- Tower work: must wear **approved fall arrest equipment**
- Ground crew: wear **hard hat** to protect against objects **dropped from tower**
- Wire antenna height: high enough **no one can touch it from the ground**
- Open-wire feedline: out of reach — **high-voltage RF can burn**
- Before antenna repairs: **turn off transmitter and disconnect transmission line**
- Ground-mounted antenna precaution: **keep people at a safe distance**

# B-002-004: Good Operating Practices — Frequency Management

## Listen Before You Transmit

The most fundamental rule in amateur radio operating: **listen first**. Before transmitting on any frequency, listen to confirm no one is already using it. Even if the frequency sounds quiet, someone may be in a pause between transmissions.

## Running Minimum Power

If a station reports your signal is extremely strong and perfectly readable, you should **reduce your power to the minimum necessary**. Strong signals occupy more dynamic range in receivers, can cause desensitization, and waste energy. Turn down power until the signal quality is just what it needs to be.

## Adjusting Your Antenna Tuner on an Active Net

If you need to tune your antenna tuner before joining an HF net that is in progress, do your tuning **3–5 kHz away from the net frequency** — not on the net frequency itself. Tuning generates noise and may cause interference if done on the active frequency.

## Dummy Loads

A **dummy load** is a resistive load (typically 50 ohms) that absorbs RF energy without radiating it. Use a dummy load when:
- Testing or adjusting your transceiver — this prevents interference to other stations
- For lengthy transmitter testing — never do extended on-air testing

The dummy load lets you drive the transmitter, measure output power, and verify operation without putting energy into the air.

## Frequency Separation for SSB

When choosing an SSB frequency near a contact already in progress, allow **approximately 3 kHz** separation to avoid mutual interference. SSB signals are typically 2–3 kHz wide, so 3 kHz puts you just clear of the adjacent signal's sideband.

## Band Plans

A **band plan** is a **voluntary** division of an amateur band into segments for different emission modes — SSB here, CW there, digital modes in this segment. Band plans minimize interference between incompatible modes (e.g., a wideband FM signal near a narrowband CW contact). They are not regulatory requirements — they are gentlemen's agreements.

## Changing Frequency When Propagation Changes

If interference increases because propagation has changed and other stations are now on your frequency, the right thing to do is **move to another frequency**.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- **Listen before transmitting** — first thing, always
- Overly strong signal: **reduce transmitter power**
- Antenna tuner adjustment near a net: **3–5 kHz away** from net frequency
- Dummy load purpose: **test/adjust without causing interference**
- SSB frequency separation from adjacent contact: **approximately 3 kHz**
- Band plan: **voluntary** division of a band to avoid mode conflicts
- First thing before transmitting: **listen carefully** so as not to interrupt existing communications

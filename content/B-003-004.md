# B-003-004: CW (Morse Code) Transmitter

## Basic CW Transmitter Architecture

A basic CW transmitter has three stages:

**Oscillator**: Generates the carrier at the transmitted frequency. In a simple CW transmitter, the output of the oscillator is directly at the transmitted signal's frequency (unlike SSB, where mixing shifts the frequency).

**Driver/buffer stage**: Amplifies the oscillator signal to the level needed by the power amplifier. Critically, the buffer **isolates the oscillator from load changes** — without it, changes in the PA loading would pull the oscillator frequency (frequency pulling). The buffer presents a stable, constant load to the oscillator.

**Power amplifier (PA)**: Increases the transmitter's output power to the final transmit level.

## The Key

The Morse key switches the carrier on and off. In a basic CW transmitter, the key controls the **amplitude of the carrier** — it gates the signal. When the key is down, the carrier is on; when up, it's off.

All stages in a CW transmitter are powered by **direct current (DC)** — the oscillator, buffer, and PA all run on DC, with the key modulating the RF output by interrupting it.

## Variable Frequency Oscillator (VFO)

Using a **variable frequency oscillator (VFO)** rather than a crystal allows operation on any frequency — not limited to the discrete frequencies available from crystals. This is the primary advantage of a VFO.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Oscillator output in a basic CW transmitter: **at the transmitted signal's frequency**
- All stages powered by: **direct current (DC)**
- Driver/buffer purpose: **prevent load changes from shifting the oscillator's frequency**
- Key function: **controls the amplitude of the carrier** (switches carrier on and off)
- PA purpose: **increases the transmitter's output power**
- VFO advantage: **frequency not constrained to available crystals**

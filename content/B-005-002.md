# B-005-002: Conductors, Insulators, and Semiconductors

## Three Categories of Materials

All materials can be classified by how readily they allow electrons to flow:

**Conductors** — materials with many free electrons that move easily under an applied voltage. Metals are conductors. Silver is the best conductor, followed by copper, then gold, then aluminium. Copper is the standard for practical wiring because of its combination of conductivity, workability, and cost.

**Insulators** — materials with very few free electrons; they strongly resist current flow. Examples: glass, rubber, mica, PTFE (Teflon), air, porcelain. Used to prevent unwanted current flow — wire insulation, standoff insulators for antennas, capacitor dielectrics.

**Semiconductors** — materials with conductivity between conductors and insulators, and crucially, conductivity that can be controlled. Silicon and germanium are the most important. Pure (intrinsic) semiconductor is a mediocre conductor, but **doping** (adding controlled impurities) dramatically changes its properties.

## N-Type and P-Type Semiconductors

**N-type**: doped with atoms that contribute extra electrons (e.g., phosphorus in silicon). Current is carried mainly by electrons (negative carriers).

**P-type**: doped with atoms that create "holes" — absences of electrons that act as positive carriers (e.g., boron in silicon). Current is carried mainly by holes.

Joining N-type and P-type material creates a **PN junction** — the basis of all diodes and transistors.

## Resistance vs. Temperature

- **Conductors**: resistance **increases** with temperature (more lattice vibration impedes electrons)
- **Semiconductors**: resistance **decreases** with temperature (more thermal energy frees carriers)
- **Thermistors**: resistors specifically designed to exploit this semiconductor temperature dependence

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Best conductor: **silver** (copper is the practical standard)
- Conductor resistance vs. temperature: **increases** with rising temperature
- Insulator examples: **glass, rubber, mica, Teflon, porcelain**
- Semiconductor resistance vs. temperature: **decreases** with rising temperature
- N-type semiconductor: majority carriers are **electrons**
- P-type semiconductor: majority carriers are **holes**
- Doping: adding impurities to **control conductivity**

# B-007-002: The Ionosphere

## What is the Ionosphere?

The **ionosphere** is the layer of the upper atmosphere (approximately 60–1000 km altitude) where solar radiation ionizes gas molecules, creating free electrons that can refract and reflect radio waves. It is the "mirror" that makes long-distance HF communication possible.

## Ionospheric Layers

The ionosphere is divided into distinct **layers**, each with different electron density and propagation characteristics:

**D Layer** (60–90 km altitude):
- Forms during daylight only; disappears at night
- **Absorbs** MF and lower HF signals (attenuates rather than reflects)
- Responsible for daytime absorption of 160 m and 80 m signals — why these bands work better at night
- HF signals pass through the D layer to reach higher layers

**E Layer** (90–130 km altitude):
- Present mainly during daylight; weakens at night
- Reflects lower HF signals (up to ≈ 20 MHz)
- **Sporadic-E (Es)**: irregular patches of high electron density that form unexpectedly and reflect VHF signals (50 MHz, 144 MHz) over long distances — the cause of sudden VHF openings

**F Layer** (150–400+ km altitude):
- The most important layer for HF sky-wave propagation
- During the day, splits into **F1** (lower) and **F2** (upper)
- At night, F1 and F2 **merge** into a single F layer
- The F2 layer has the highest electron density and can reflect the highest frequencies
- Responsible for intercontinental HF communication (thousands of km)

## Why Night Propagation Differs from Day

- **D layer** disappears at night → lower HF (40 m, 80 m) is no longer absorbed; sky-wave propagation on these bands becomes possible
- **F1 and F2 merge** → single, effective reflecting layer
- This is why 40 m and 80 m become long-distance DX bands at night

## Exam-Critical Facts

- D layer altitude: **60–90 km** — present during **daylight only**
- D layer function: **absorbs** MF and lower HF (causes daytime attenuation on 80 m, 160 m)
- E layer altitude: **90–130 km**
- Sporadic-E: irregular **E layer** patches that reflect **VHF**
- F layer altitude: **150–400+ km** — most important for **HF DX**
- F layer splits into **F1 and F2** during daytime; merges at **night**
- F2 layer: highest electron density — reflects **highest frequencies**
- Night advantage: D layer gone → lower HF bands open for **sky-wave DX**

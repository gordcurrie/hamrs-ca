# B-008-001: Interference — Causes and Types

## What is Interference?

**Interference** (RFI — Radio Frequency Interference, or EMI — Electromagnetic Interference) occurs when an unwanted signal degrades the reception of a wanted signal, or when a transmitter affects equipment that was not intended to receive radio signals (TVs, audio equipment, telephones).

As an amateur radio operator, you may both **cause** interference and **receive** interference. Resolving both directions is a core operating skill.

## Types of Interference You May Cause

**Fundamental overload**: Your transmitter's fundamental signal (the actual operating frequency) is simply too strong and overpowers adjacent receivers or equipment. The receiving equipment is picking up your signal on your actual operating frequency — not a spurious emission. This is not your fault technically but must still be addressed.

**Harmonic interference**: Your transmitter radiates energy at multiples of your operating frequency (harmonics) that fall on other services (TV channels, FM broadcast). This IS a transmitter fault — spurious emissions must be suppressed.

**Spurious emissions**: Any unwanted signal from your transmitter other than the intended fundamental — includes harmonics, intermodulation products, parasitic oscillations. Regulations require these to be kept below specified levels.

**Intermodulation (IMD)**: When two or more strong signals mix in a non-linear device (a transmitter PA, or even a corroded joint in your antenna system), they produce sum and difference frequencies that can fall on other channels.

## Types of Interference You May Receive

**Broadcast interference**: Strong AM/FM broadcast stations overload your receiver front end, causing spurious signals throughout the HF spectrum.

**Line noise**: Power lines, motors, LED dimmers, switching power supplies, and solar inverters generate broadband noise that raises your noise floor.

**Digital device interference**: Computers, network switches, LED lights, and USB devices radiate RF that can be received on HF antennas.

## Who is Responsible?

- If the problem is **your spurious emissions** (harmonics) → fix your station
- If the problem is **fundamental overload of poorly designed consumer equipment** → technically their equipment's problem, but cooperation is expected
- The Radio Amateurs of Canada (RAC) and ISED provide guidance on resolving RFI disputes

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Fundamental overload: receiver is picking up your **fundamental frequency** (not your fault technically)
- Harmonic interference: your transmitter radiates at **multiples of operating frequency** (your problem to fix)
- Spurious emissions: any **unintended transmitter output** other than fundamental
- IMD: mixing of two signals in a **non-linear device** produces spurious products
- Line noise: common source is **power lines, switching supplies, LED dimmers**
- Amateur's responsibility: keep **spurious emissions** below regulatory limits

# B-006-006: Dipole Antennas

## What is a Dipole?

A **dipole antenna** is two conductors extending in opposite directions from the feedpoint, each approximately one quarter-wavelength long — making the total antenna approximately **one half-wavelength (λ/2)** long. It is the most fundamental and commonly used antenna.

## Half-Wave Dipole Length

The approximate length of a half-wave dipole in free space:

> **Length (metres) = 150 / f(MHz)**   (for a full half-wave)

In practice, the antenna is slightly shorter than a theoretical half-wave due to the **velocity factor** of wire and end effects. A commonly used practical formula:

> **Length (metres) ≈ 143 / f(MHz)**

For a single quarter-wave element:

> **Length (metres) ≈ 71.5 / f(MHz)**

**Example**: 7 MHz dipole: 143 / 7 ≈ **20.4 metres total** (each leg ≈ 10.2 m)

## Feedpoint Impedance

A half-wave dipole in free space has a feedpoint impedance of approximately **72 Ω** — close enough to 50 Ω coax for a reasonable direct match (SWR ≈ 1.4:1). The exact impedance varies with height above ground.

## Radiation Pattern

A horizontal half-wave dipole radiates **broadside** — maximum radiation is perpendicular to the wire, with nulls off the ends. The pattern is **bidirectional** (figure-eight shape viewed from above).

## Centre-Fed vs. End-Fed

- **Centre-fed dipole**: feedline connects at the centre; feedpoint impedance ≈ 72 Ω; common configuration
- **End-fed half-wave (EFHW)**: feedline at one end; very high feedpoint impedance (2000–5000 Ω); requires a matching transformer

## Inverted-V Dipole

An **inverted-V** has the feedpoint at the top (on a mast) and the legs sloping down at angles. This reduces the height needed for the supports and provides a slightly different radiation pattern. The feedpoint impedance drops toward 50 Ω as the angle between the legs decreases.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Dipole length: approximately **λ/2** (half-wavelength)
- Practical length formula: **143 / f(MHz)** metres total
- Feedpoint: connected at the **centre**
- Feedpoint impedance: approximately **72 Ω** (in free space)
- Radiation: maximum **broadside** (perpendicular to wire)
- Dipole radiation pattern: **bidirectional** (figure-eight)
- For 14 MHz: 143/14 ≈ **10.2 metres total**

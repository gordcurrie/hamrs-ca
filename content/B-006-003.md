# B-006-003: SWR — Standing Wave Ratio

## What is SWR?

**Standing Wave Ratio (SWR)** — also **VSWR (Voltage Standing Wave Ratio)** — is a measure of how well the antenna's impedance matches the transmission line's characteristic impedance. It is the ratio of maximum to minimum voltage amplitude along the line.

A perfect match → **SWR = 1:1** (no reflected power, all energy reaches the antenna)  
Any mismatch → SWR > 1:1 (some power reflected back toward the transmitter)

## What Causes High SWR?

High SWR results from a **mismatch between the antenna feedpoint impedance and the transmission line impedance**. Common causes:
- Antenna cut to wrong length (not resonant at the operating frequency)
- Wrong feedpoint impedance (antenna designed for 75 Ω fed with 50 Ω coax)
- Damaged or wet feedline connector
- Antenna too close to objects that detune it

## Effects of High SWR

- **Reflected power**: energy travels back toward the transmitter
- **Increased feedline loss**: reflected waves add to cable heating
- **Transmitter stress**: many modern transceivers automatically reduce power output when SWR exceeds ~2:1–3:1 to protect the PA transistors
- **Reduced efficiency**: less power reaches the antenna

## Acceptable SWR

A **2:1 SWR** is widely accepted as the upper limit for normal operation — at 2:1, about 11% of the power is reflected (89% reaches the antenna). Many transceivers tolerate up to **3:1** before folding back.

## SWR Meter

An **SWR meter** (directional power meter) connects in series with the feedline. It samples forward and reflected power and displays the ratio. Place it **at the transmitter end** of the feedline.

## The Relationship Between SWR and Antenna Tuner

An **antenna tuner** (ATU / transmatch) transforms the impedance seen at the shack end of the feedline to 50 Ω, protecting the transmitter. However, if SWR on the feedline is very high, the feedline loss itself increases — the ATU does not reduce loss in the feedline.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- SWR = 1:1 means: **perfect match, no reflected power**
- SWR cause: **impedance mismatch** between antenna and feedline
- High SWR effect on transmitter: many transceivers **reduce power output**
- SWR effect on feedline: **increases line loss**
- Acceptable maximum SWR for normal operation: **2:1 to 3:1**
- SWR meter position: **between transmitter and antenna** (in series)
- Antenna tuner: matches impedance to protect transmitter but **does not reduce feedline loss**

# B-003-011: Modulation Basics, Chirp, and Transmitter Efficiency

## Amplitude Modulation (AM)

In AM, the **instantaneous amplitude (envelope)** of the RF signal varies with the modulating audio. The RF carrier is present at all times; the audio signal varies its amplitude above and below the carrier level.

An AM signal carrying a single audio tone has three frequency components: **carrier and two sidebands** (one above, one below the carrier frequency). If the highest voice frequency is 3 kHz, the total AM bandwidth is **6 kHz** (3 kHz below and 3 kHz above the carrier).

**Morse code** transmitted by radio is an interrupted carrier — the carrier is switched on and off. Technically this is OOK (on-off keying), a form of amplitude modulation where the amplitude goes to zero when the key is up.

## Frequency Stability and Chirp

An oscillator must be **electrically and mechanically stable** to avoid drifting in frequency over time. Temperature changes, mechanical vibration, and variations in supply voltage can all shift the oscillator frequency.

**Chirp** is a specific problem in CW transmitters: a small change in the output frequency each time a dit or dah is sent. Cause: the current drawn by the key-down PA stage loads the power supply, causing a slight voltage drop, which shifts the oscillator frequency. The signal "chirps" up or down at the start of each element.

**Fix**: Keep the power supply voltages **very steady under varying loads** — good regulation. A regulated supply that holds voltage constant during transmit/receive switching eliminates chirp.

## Transmitter Efficiency

Power in = DC input power to the final RF stage
Power out = RF output power

The difference (input - output) **appears as heat** — it is dissipated in the transistors or tubes of the PA stage. This is why power amplifiers need heat sinks.

Example: DC input 200 W, RF output 125 W → 75 W dissipated as heat. Efficiency = 125/200 = 62.5%.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- AM: instantaneous **amplitude (envelope)** of RF varies with audio
- AM bandwidth with 3 kHz audio: **6 kHz** (carrier + two sidebands)
- AM components: **carrier and two sidebands**
- Morse code by radio: **interrupted carrier**
- Chirp: **small change in output frequency** each time a dit or dah is sent
- Chirp fix: **keep power supply voltages very steady** under varying loads
- VFO advantage over crystal: **frequency not constrained to available crystals**
- Power difference (DC input − RF output): **appears as heat**
- Oscillator must be stable to prevent: **drifting in frequency over time**

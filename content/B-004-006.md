# B-004-006: Resistor Colour Codes

## Why Colour Codes?

Small resistors are too small to print a readable number on. Instead, coloured bands encode the value. Learn the sequence and you can read any standard resistor.

## The Colour Sequence

The standard mnemonic: **B**lack **B**rown **R**ed **O**range **Y**ellow **G**reen **B**lue **V**iolet **G**rey **W**hite

| Colour | Digit | Multiplier |
|--------|-------|------------|
| Black  | 0     | ×1         |
| Brown  | 1     | ×10        |
| Red    | 2     | ×100       |
| Orange | 3     | ×1 000     |
| Yellow | 4     | ×10 000    |
| Green  | 5     | ×100 000   |
| Blue   | 6     | ×1 000 000 |
| Violet | 7     | —          |
| Grey   | 8     | —          |
| White  | 9     | —          |

## Tolerance Band

A fourth band (or fifth on precision resistors) indicates tolerance:
- **Gold**: ±5%
- **Silver**: ±10%
- **No band**: ±20%

## Reading a Four-Band Resistor

1. Band 1 — first digit
2. Band 2 — second digit
3. Band 3 — multiplier (number of zeros)
4. Band 4 — tolerance

**Example**: Yellow–Violet–Red–Gold = 4, 7, ×100, ±5% = **4700 Ω (4.7 kΩ) ±5%**

**Example**: Brown–Black–Orange–Gold = 1, 0, ×1000, ±5% = **10 000 Ω (10 kΩ) ±5%**

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Colour sequence: **Black(0) Brown(1) Red(2) Orange(3) Yellow(4) Green(5) Blue(6) Violet(7) Grey(8) White(9)**
- Band 1: **first digit**
- Band 2: **second digit**
- Band 3: **multiplier** (power of 10)
- Band 4: **tolerance** (Gold=±5%, Silver=±10%)
- Yellow–Violet–Red = **4 700 Ω**
- Brown–Black–Red = **1 000 Ω (1 kΩ)**

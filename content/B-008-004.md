# B-008-004: Receiver Interference and Overload

## Receiver Overload (Front-End Overload)

**Front-end overload** occurs when a very strong signal — not necessarily on your operating frequency — overpowers the receiver's preamplifier or mixer. The receiver is driven into non-linearity, and instead of filtering out the strong signal, it generates spurious responses throughout the spectrum.

Symptoms:
- Many stations suddenly appear when a strong local station transmits
- Noise floor jumps when a nearby transmitter keys up
- Signals appear that don't exist when the strong interferer is absent

The cause is the **receiver's RF stage being overloaded**, not anything wrong with the transmitting station.

## Solutions for Receiver Overload

- **Reduce receiver gain**: turn down the RF gain control
- **Attenuator**: insert a fixed attenuator (6 dB or 10 dB) ahead of the receiver to reduce all signals
- **Band-pass filter**: a band-pass filter (BPF) ahead of the receiver passes only the amateur band of interest and rejects everything else — the most effective solution
- **Improve receiver selectivity**: some receivers have a preamp that can be switched off

## Intermodulation in Receivers

When two strong signals A and B are applied to a non-linear receiver front end, **intermodulation products** are generated at frequencies like 2A−B and 2B−A. These products may fall in the receiver's passband and sound like legitimate signals.

**Example**: Two broadcast stations at 100 MHz and 102 MHz produce an IMD product at 98 MHz — which happens to fall in the 2 m band if you're using a receiver tuned there.

The **third-order intercept point (IP3)** is a measure of how well a receiver resists this effect — higher IP3 = more IMD-resistant.

## Image Rejection

A **superheterodyne receiver** converts the desired signal to an intermediate frequency (IF) by mixing with a local oscillator (LO). Signals at a frequency **exactly 2 × IF away** from the desired signal also mix to produce output at the IF — these are **image signals**. A band-pass filter before the mixer (the "preselector" or "image reject filter") suppresses image signals.

## Desensitization

A strong signal can reduce the gain of a receiver's RF amplifier for all signals — the amplifier is operating in a compressed region. This is **desensitization** — legitimate weak signals disappear when a strong one appears.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Front-end overload: strong signal **drives receiver into non-linearity**, causing spurious signals
- Overload cause: **receiver problem** — not the transmitting station's fault
- Best fix for overload: **band-pass filter** ahead of receiver
- Attenuator: reduces **all signals** — simple but sacrifices weak signals too
- Intermodulation: mixing of **two strong signals** in a non-linear receiver stage
- IMD products at: **2A−B and 2B−A** (third-order products)
- Image frequency: signal **2 × IF away** from desired — rejected by preselector filter
- Desensitization: strong signal **reduces receiver gain** for all other signals

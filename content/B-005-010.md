# B-005-010: Reactance, Impedance, and Phase

## Reactance

**Reactance (X)** is the opposition to AC current caused by inductors and capacitors. Unlike resistance, reactance does not dissipate energy — it stores and returns it.

- **Inductive reactance**: X_L = 2πfL (ohms) — increases with frequency
- **Capacitive reactance**: X_C = 1/(2πfC) (ohms) — decreases with frequency

## Impedance

**Impedance (Z)** is the total opposition to AC current — the combination of resistance (R) and reactance (X):

> **Z = √(R² + X²)**

Impedance is measured in **ohms (Ω)**. It accounts for both the resistive (in-phase) and reactive (out-of-phase) components.

When only R is present: Z = R  
When only X is present: Z = X  
Mixed: use the formula above.

## Phase Relationships

In a **purely resistive** circuit: voltage and current are **in phase** (no angle between them).

In a **purely inductive** circuit: voltage **leads** current by 90° (ELI — **E**MF **L**eads **I**n inductor).

In a **purely capacitive** circuit: current **leads** voltage by 90° (ICE — **I**n **C**apacitor, current leads **E**MF).

Mnemonic: **ELI the ICE man** — in an inductor (L), E leads I; in a capacitor (C), I leads E.

## Power Factor

In a reactive circuit, not all of the apparent power (V × I) does useful work. The **power factor** is the ratio of real power (watts) to apparent power (volt-amperes):

> Power factor = cos(θ) where θ is the phase angle between V and I.

A purely resistive circuit has a power factor of 1. A purely reactive circuit has a power factor of 0.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Reactance: opposition to AC from **inductors and capacitors** (no energy dissipated)
- Impedance: **Z = √(R² + X²)**, measured in ohms
- Inductive reactance increases with: **frequency**
- Capacitive reactance decreases with: **frequency**
- Voltage leads current in: **inductors** (ELI)
- Current leads voltage in: **capacitors** (ICE)
- Mnemonic: **ELI the ICE man**
- Pure resistor: voltage and current are **in phase**

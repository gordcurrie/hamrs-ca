# B-008-005: Filters for Interference Suppression

## The Role of Filters

**Filters** are the primary technical tool for resolving interference problems — both at transmitters (to suppress spurious emissions) and at receivers (to reject unwanted signals). The right filter in the right location solves most interference problems.

## Low-Pass Filter (LPF)

A **low-pass filter** passes frequencies **below** a cut-off frequency and attenuates frequencies above it.

**Use at the transmitter output**: An LPF with cut-off just above the highest amateur band in use will pass the fundamental signal but attenuate harmonics (which are at higher frequencies). This is the standard solution for harmonic interference to TV and FM broadcast.

## High-Pass Filter (HPF)

A **high-pass filter** passes frequencies **above** a cut-off frequency and attenuates frequencies below it.

**Use at affected TV or FM equipment**: An HPF with cut-off around 50 MHz will block HF transmissions (below 30 MHz) from entering TV or FM equipment via the antenna input. This protects the TV receiver from HF fundamental overload.

## Band-Pass Filter (BPF)

A **band-pass filter** passes a **range of frequencies** and attenuates signals above and below that range.

**Use at receiver input**: A BPF tuned to the amateur band of interest rejects out-of-band signals — broadcast stations, cellular, etc. — before they reach the front end. Most effective solution for front-end overload.

## Band-Stop Filter (Notch Filter / BSF)

A **band-stop filter** (also called a **notch filter**) attenuates a specific narrow range of frequencies and passes everything else. It is the opposite of a band-pass filter.

**Use**: Eliminate a specific interfering signal (e.g., a broadcast station) from the receive path without affecting the rest of the band.

## Ferrite Chokes (Common-Mode Choke)

A **ferrite choke** is not a frequency-selective filter — it attenuates RF on the outside of cables regardless of frequency. Used to:
- Prevent RF from traveling along the outside of coax braid (common-mode current)
- Suppress audio rectification by choking RF on audio leads
- Improve balun action at antenna feedpoints

Multiple turns of coax through a large ferrite toroid form a common-mode choke with high impedance to common-mode RF.

## Summary: Filter Placement

| Problem | Filter Type | Location |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Harmonic interference to TV | Low-pass | Transmitter output |
| HF overload of TV/FM | High-pass | TV/FM antenna input |
| Receiver front-end overload | Band-pass | Receiver input |
| Single interferer elimination | Band-stop/notch | Receiver input |
| Audio rectification | Ferrite choke | Affected audio cables |

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Low-pass filter at transmitter: suppresses **harmonics** (used to protect TV/FM from harmonics)
- High-pass filter at TV/FM: blocks **HF fundamental** from entering consumer equipment
- Band-pass filter at receiver: rejects **out-of-band signals** (best fix for overload)
- Notch/band-stop filter: eliminates a **specific interfering frequency**
- Ferrite choke: attenuates **common-mode RF** on cables (not frequency-selective)
- Cut-off frequency of LPF for HF transmitter: just **above the operating band**
- Cut-off frequency of HPF for TV protection: approximately **50 MHz**

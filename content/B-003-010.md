# B-003-010: Receiver Performance and Bandwidth

## Bandwidth vs. Mode

Different emission modes require different bandwidths. From narrowest to widest:

**CW < SSB voice < FM voice**

- CW: ~100–500 Hz (a pure interrupted carrier; the bandwidth depends on keying speed and filter choice)
- SSB: ~2.4 kHz (voice frequency range 300–3000 Hz, one sideband)
- FM voice: ~10–20 kHz (with 5 kHz deviation; wider than SSB because FM encodes information in frequency swing)

## Receiver Sensitivity

Sensitivity is the ability to receive weak signals. It is specified as the **minimum RF input signal needed to achieve a given signal-to-noise ratio** at the audio output. A more sensitive receiver can pull out weaker signals.

## Selectivity and Filters

Selectivity is the ability to reject adjacent-channel interference. The IF filter determines this.

Choosing the right filter for the mode:
- **SSB**: 2.4 kHz filter — matches the SSB signal bandwidth
- **CW with interference**: 250 Hz filter — very narrow, rejects nearly all adjacent signals; the trade-off is that the tone must be very accurate to stay in the passband
- **CW standard**: optimum receive range is 750–850 Hz centre frequency

Too narrow a filter: **loss of information** (parts of the signal get cut off)
Too wide a filter: more interference passes through

## Dynamic Range

The three main indicators of communications receiver performance:
1. **Sensitivity** — how weak a signal can be received
2. **Selectivity** — how well adjacent signals are rejected
3. **Dynamic range** — how well the receiver handles large signal differences without generating intermodulation or desensitization

## Waterfall Display

A modern software-defined radio or digital receiver displays a **waterfall** (spectrogram). It shows two parameters simultaneously: **amplitude** (signal strength, shown as colour/brightness) and **frequency** (horizontal position). Time runs downward, creating the "waterfall" appearance.

## Superheterodyne IF Calculation

IF = |LO − RF| (assuming local oscillator runs above the received frequency):

Example: IF = 455 kHz, receiving 3.54 MHz, LO runs above:
LO = 3.54 MHz + 0.455 MHz = **3.995 MHz**

## SSB Tuning Accuracy

SSB requires accurate tuning **within about 100 Hz**. The missing carrier means the receiver's BFO must substitute for it; even a 100 Hz error is audible as pitch shift. CW is similar. AM and FM are much more tolerant of tuning errors.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Bandwidth order, narrowest to widest: **CW < SSB < FM**
- Sensitivity: **minimum RF input for a given signal-to-noise ratio**
- SSB filter: **2.4 kHz**; CW with interference: **250 Hz**; CW optimum: **750–850 Hz**
- Too narrow bandwidth: **loss of information**
- Third performance indicator (beyond sensitivity/selectivity): **dynamic range**
- Waterfall display shows: **amplitude and frequency**
- SSB tuning accuracy: **within 100 Hz**
- LO calculation: IF = 455 kHz, 3.54 MHz → LO = **3.995 MHz**

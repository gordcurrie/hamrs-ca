# B-007-005: VHF/UHF Propagation — Tropospheric and Enhanced Modes

## Normal VHF/UHF Propagation

Under normal conditions, VHF and UHF signals propagate by **line of sight** — they travel in a nearly straight line and are limited by the radio horizon (slightly beyond the optical horizon due to atmospheric refraction).

Typical VHF/UHF range: **50–150 km** for modest antenna heights.

## Tropospheric Propagation

The **troposphere** (0–10 km altitude, the weather layer) can bend or trap radio waves and extend VHF/UHF range far beyond line of sight.

### Tropospheric Ducting

A **tropospheric duct** forms when a temperature inversion creates a layer where temperature increases with altitude instead of decreasing. This creates a low-loss waveguide that traps RF energy and guides it along the surface. Signals can travel **hundreds to thousands of kilometres** with very little loss.

Most common conditions: **anticyclones (high pressure systems)**, especially in summer. Coastal regions experience ducting more often than inland areas. Common on 2 m, 70 cm, and 23 cm.

### Tropospheric Scatter

**Troposcatter** uses scattering from inhomogeneities (turbulence) in the troposphere. The signal is scattered forward from the common volume visible to both stations. Range: **200–1000 km**. Not as dramatic as ducting, but reliable.

## Sporadic-E

**Sporadic-E** propagation occurs on VHF (especially 50 MHz, sometimes 144 MHz) when patches of intense ionization appear in the E layer. These are unpredictable but can produce strong, 1000–2000 km openings lasting minutes to hours.

Most common: late spring and early summer in the northern hemisphere (May–July).

## Aurora

Energetic particles from the sun interact with the earth's magnetic field near the poles, producing the **aurora**. The ionized aurora can scatter and reflect VHF signals — enabling contacts between stations in the northern parts of the continent during geomagnetic storms.

Aurora contacts have a distinctive **rough, buzzy sound** due to the rapid movement of the auroral curtain.

## Moonbounce (EME)

**Earth-Moon-Earth (EME)** propagation bounces signals off the moon. The path length is approximately 760,000 km round-trip. Requires high power and large antennas. Enables worldwide contacts on VHF/UHF with no ionospheric conditions required.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Normal VHF/UHF mode: **line of sight**
- Tropospheric duct: caused by **temperature inversion** (high pressure system)
- Ducting range: **hundreds to thousands of km**
- Sporadic-E: patches of intense **E-layer ionization** → unexpected VHF openings
- Sporadic-E most common: **late spring / early summer** (May–July)
- Aurora propagation: **geomagnetic storm** energizes ionized aurora
- Aurora signal characteristic: **rough, buzzy audio**
- EME: signals bounce off the **moon** for worldwide VHF/UHF contacts

# B-003-012: SSB Operating Parameters and Signal Quality

## Peak Envelope Power (PEP)

**PEP (Peak Envelope Power)** is the average power during one RF cycle at the **crest of the modulation envelope**. For SSB, power varies continuously with voice — PEP measures the highest power level reached during peaks of speech.

PEP is the regulatory limit for SSB transmitters (not average power, not carrier power).

## SSB Signal Bandwidth

A typical SSB signal occupies **between 2 kHz and 3 kHz** — the voice audio range from roughly 300 Hz to 2700 Hz on one sideband.

## ALC — Automatic Level Control

The **ALC (Automatic Level Control)** circuit limits the input audio peaks so the transmitter is **not overdriven**. ALC monitors the PA output level and feeds back a control voltage to reduce the microphone or driver gain when the signal is too strong. Proper adjustment: keep the maximum ALC meter deflection within the normal range on voice peaks — never pegged.

## Overmodulation Effects

When an SSB transmitter is overdriven (microphone gain too high, too much speech processing, or ALC not functioning):
- The signal becomes **distorted**
- It **occupies more bandwidth** than it should — this is called "splatter"
- Adjacent stations are interfered with

The output of a speech processor is to increase **average power** without increasing peak power — effectively compressing the dynamic range. At 100% modulation, a speech processor can increase average power relative to unprocessed speech. The trade-off is potential distortion if pushed too far.

## Balanced Modulator

In an SSB transmitter, the balanced modulator's purpose: **suppress the carrier and pass the two sidebands**. It is the stage that generates DSB-SC (double sideband, suppressed carrier) from which one sideband is then filtered.

## SSB Sideband Calculation

SSB transmits only one sideband. For LSB (lower sideband) at 7100 kHz with a 1000 Hz tone:
- The lower sideband of a 1000 Hz tone at 7100 kHz carrier = 7100 kHz − 1 kHz = **7099 kHz**

## PA Linearity

The SSB power amplifier must be **linear** — amplitude variations in the SSB signal carry the voice information. A non-linear PA distorts these variations, making the transmitted voice unintelligible.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- PEP: average power at the **crest of the modulation envelope**
- SSB bandwidth: **2–3 kHz**
- ALC: limits audio peaks so transmitter is **not overdriven**
- Set microphone gain: so **ALC meter maximum range is not exceeded on voice peaks**
- Overmodulation: signal **distorted and occupies more bandwidth** (splatter)
- Speech processor at 100% modulation: **increases average power**
- Balanced modulator: **suppresses carrier and passes two sidebands**
- LSB at 7100 kHz with 1000 Hz tone: RF at **7099 kHz**
- SSB PA must be: **linear** (non-linear → unintelligible audio)

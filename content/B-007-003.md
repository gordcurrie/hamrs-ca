# B-007-003: Skip Distance and Sky-Wave Propagation

## Sky-Wave Propagation

**Sky-wave propagation** occurs when HF radio waves are directed upward at an angle, refracted (bent) by the ionosphere, and returned to earth at a distant point. The wave is not reflected like a mirror — it is gradually bent by the progressively denser ionized layer until it curves back down.

## Skip Distance

**Skip distance** is the minimum ground distance from the transmitting station at which a sky-wave signal will return to earth for a given frequency. Between the transmitter and the inner edge of the skip zone, the signal arrives only by ground wave (which fades rapidly with distance on HF).

## The Skip Zone

The **skip zone** (or dead zone) is the area where **no signal is received** — the ground wave has faded out but the sky-wave has not yet returned to earth. A station inside another station's skip zone cannot hear them on that frequency. The skip zone is typically:
- Closer to the transmitter for higher frequencies
- Smaller or nonexistent on lower HF bands (where ground wave persists longer)

## Angle of Radiation

The **takeoff angle** (angle of radiation) determines how far the sky-wave skip goes:
- **Low angle** (near horizontal): skip travels farther before returning — for long-distance DX
- **High angle** (steep): returns sooner — for shorter distances

High-gain, low-angle antennas (horizontal Yagi at height) optimize for DX. An NVIS (Near Vertical Incidence Skywave) antenna radiates straight up and covers short ranges (500–3000 km) — useful for regional emergency communication.

## Multiple Hops

A signal can **bounce multiple times** between the ionosphere and earth, extending range beyond a single hop. Each bounce attenuates the signal, but **multi-hop propagation** allows intercontinental contacts on HF.

## The MUF and LUF

**MUF (Maximum Usable Frequency)**: the highest frequency that will return to earth via the ionosphere for a given path and ionospheric conditions. Above the MUF, signals pass through the ionosphere into space.

**LUF (Lowest Usable Frequency)**: the lowest frequency that will propagate adequately via sky wave — lower frequencies suffer more D-layer absorption.

The optimal operating frequency is **just below the MUF**.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Skip zone: region of **no signal** — beyond ground wave, before sky-wave return
- Skip distance: minimum distance at which **sky-wave returns to earth**
- Low radiation angle: for **long-distance DX**
- NVIS: very high radiation angle — for **regional communication** (500–3000 km)
- MUF: highest frequency that **ionosphere will return** to earth
- Above MUF: signals **pass through ionosphere** (lost to space)
- LUF: lowest frequency with acceptable sky-wave propagation (D-layer limits)
- Best operating frequency: **just below the MUF**

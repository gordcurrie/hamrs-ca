# B-005-004: Electrical Power

## Power Formula

**Electrical power** is the rate at which energy is consumed or delivered:

**P = V × I**

Where:
- **P** = power in watts (W)
- **V** = voltage in volts (V)
- **I** = current in amperes (A)

Combined with Ohm's Law (V = IR), two more useful forms:

**P = I² × R** (when you know current and resistance)

**P = V² / R** (when you know voltage and resistance)

## The Watt

Power is measured in **watts (W)**. Related units:
- **milliwatt (mW)** = 0.001 W
- **kilowatt (kW)** = 1000 W
- 1 watt = 1 joule per second (energy per unit time)

## Worked Examples

**Example 1**: 12 V at 5 A = 12 × 5 = **60 W**

**Example 2**: 100 mA (= 0.1 A) through 470 Ω = (0.1)² × 470 = 0.01 × 470 = **4.7 W**

**Example 3**: 120 V across 240 Ω = 120² / 240 = 14400 / 240 = **60 W**

## Power and Heat

Power not delivered as useful output is dissipated as **heat**. This is why resistors have power ratings — a 1/4 W resistor will overheat and fail if you push 1 W through it. Always check that component power ratings are not exceeded.

## RF Power Output

In amateur radio:
- **PEP (Peak Envelope Power)**: the power averaged over one RF cycle at the peak of the modulation envelope — the standard measure for SSB transmitters
- **Average power**: relevant for carrier modes (FM, CW)

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Power formula: **P = V × I**
- Alternative: **P = I² × R** and **P = V² / R**
- Unit of power: **watts (W)**
- 13.8 V at 10 A: P = 13.8 × 10 = **138 W**
- Power dissipated as: **heat** (when not useful output)
- PEP: power measured at **peak of modulation envelope** (SSB standard)
- Doubling current with same resistance: power increases **4×** (P = I²R)

# B-007-007: Fading, Multipath, and Propagation Effects

## Signal Fading

**Fading** is a variation in received signal strength over time. On HF, fading is caused by:

**Multipath fading**: Signals arrive at the receiver via **multiple paths** — direct ground wave, sky wave via F1, sky wave via F2, multi-hop, etc. When these signals are slightly out of phase, they **partially cancel** (destructive interference). As ionospheric conditions change, the phase relationships change, causing the signal to fluctuate. This is **selective fading** — it may affect some frequencies in the transmitted signal more than others.

**Selective fading** on SSB: Different components of the voice signal are attenuated by different amounts, causing distortion. This is why SSB can become unintelligible during propagation.

## Flutter and Doppler Spread

When reflecting layers are moving or irregular (common during geomagnetic disturbances), the signal undergoes **flutter** — rapid amplitude variations. The movement of the reflecting region introduces **Doppler shift**, spreading the signal slightly in frequency.

## Multipath at VHF/UHF

At VHF and UHF (particularly for mobile stations), **multipath** occurs when signals reflect off buildings, hills, and other structures, arriving via multiple paths. When these paths have different lengths, the signals can cancel:
- **Standing waves** in a room from a static reflector
- **Mobile flutter**: rapid cancellations as a moving vehicle changes the path geometry — the familiar rapid "motor-boating" sound in mobile FM

## Picket Fencing

**Picket fencing** is rapid signal strength fluctuation experienced by a mobile station — named for the sound as signal strength pulses in and out as the vehicle moves through areas of constructive and destructive interference. Common at VHF/UHF.

## Long Path and Short Path

For any two points on earth, radio waves can travel two routes: the **short path** (direct) and the **long path** (going around the other side). Long path contacts are possible when ionospheric conditions support propagation on that route (typically opposite the short-path direction by 180°).

## Exam-Critical Facts

- HF fading cause: **multipath** signals arriving with varying phase (destructive interference)
- Selective fading: affects **different parts of the signal differently** — distorts SSB
- Mobile VHF fading: **multipath reflections** off buildings and terrain
- Picket fencing: rapid fading due to **constructive/destructive interference** as mobile station moves
- Flutter: rapid variation from **moving or irregular reflecting layers**
- Long path: 180° opposite of short path; sometimes better propagation route

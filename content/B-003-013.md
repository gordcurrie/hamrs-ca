# B-003-013: FM Characteristics and Operation

## FM Deviation

In FM, the audio signal varies the **frequency** of the carrier. The amount of frequency shift from the centre frequency is called **deviation**. A louder (higher amplitude) audio signal causes greater deviation; a softer signal causes less.

**Bandwidth of an FM signal**: approximately 2 × (deviation + highest audio frequency). For 5 kHz deviation with voice audio: roughly **10–20 kHz** occupied bandwidth. This is why FM is not used on HF amateur bands where bandwidth limits (typically 6 kHz) would be far exceeded.

## Deviation Mismatch Effects

If you transmit into a repeater configured for 5 kHz deviation using a radio set to 2.5 kHz deviation (half the expected):
- Your audio will be **low** — quieter than normal at the repeater output
- Not distorted, just low — the repeater's deemphasis and audio chain expect 5 kHz deviation worth of audio swing

## The FM Capture Effect

When more than one signal is present on an FM frequency, the FM receiver tends to **demodulate only the strongest signal**. This is the **capture effect** — a strong signal "captures" the demodulator and suppresses weaker interferers. It works both ways: you get cleaner reception of the dominant signal, but the weaker station is completely unheard.

## FM Noise Behaviour

FM receivers have characteristic noise behaviour:
- With a strong signal: quiet, clean audio (FM's noise rejection at work)
- With no signal or a very weak signal: **loud noise** from the AF stages. The cause is the **very large gain** of stages ahead of the discriminator amplifying noise to full level. The limiter and discriminator are essentially demodulating noise when no carrier is present.

The **squelch** circuit (in the AF amplifier) mutes the output when signal is absent, suppressing this noise.

## Unmodulated Carrier

If an FM transmitter's microphone fails: the output is an **unmodulated carrier** — FM with zero deviation. The carrier is present but carrying no information.

## Phase Modulation

FM and PM (phase modulation) are closely related. In FM, frequency varies with audio; in PM, phase varies with audio. The waveforms are similar, and many "FM" transmitters are actually phase modulators. Phase modulation is the **modulation method most closely related to FM**.

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Deviation: **change in frequency caused by modulation**
- FM bandwidth with 5 kHz deviation: **10–20 kHz**
- FM not used below 28 MHz: **bandwidth exceeds regulations**
- Transmitting at 2.5 kHz deviation into a 5 kHz system: **audio will be low**
- Capture effect: FM receiver demodulates **only the strongest signal**
- No-signal FM noise cause: **very large gain of stages ahead of the discriminator**
- Failed microphone FM output: **unmodulated carrier**
- Higher modulation level in FM: **larger deviation of the carrier frequency**
- Mode most related to FM: **phase modulation**

# B-002-007: Q Codes

## What Q Codes Are

Q codes are a system of three-letter codes beginning with Q that originated in early Morse code communication to convey common messages with minimum characters. They are still widely used in amateur radio, especially on CW and in nets.

A Q code used as a statement means "I am experiencing X" or "Do X." A Q code with a question mark (?) means "Are you experiencing X?" or "Please do X."

## Essential Q Codes for the Exam

| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **QRL?** | Is this frequency in use? (proper way to check before transmitting on CW) |
| **QRM** | I am being interfered with (man-made interference) |
| **QRN** | I am troubled by static (natural interference, atmospheric noise) |
| **QRS** | Send more slowly |
| **QRX** | I will call you again (stand by) |
| **QRZ?** | Who is calling me? / Who is calling? |
| **QSB** | Your signal is fading |
| **QSY** | Change to another frequency |
| **QTH** | My location is... |

## How to Use QRL?

Before transmitting on a CW frequency, the correct procedure is to send **QRL?** — "Is this frequency in use?" This is better than just listening, because a contact may be in a temporary pause. If someone responds, the frequency is in use.

## Distinction: QRM vs. QRN

- **QRM**: interference from other stations or man-made noise (radio frequency interference)
- **QRN**: interference from static, atmospheric conditions, or natural electrical noise

- QRS: **send more slowly**

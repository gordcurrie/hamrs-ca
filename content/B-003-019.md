# B-003-019: Station Grounding and Electrical Safety

## Why Ground All Station Equipment

For best protection from electrical shock, **all station equipment** should be grounded — connected to a common earth ground. This ensures that if a fault occurs (e.g., the mains live wire contacts the chassis), the chassis does not become energized at mains voltage. Instead, the fault current flows safely to ground, blowing the fuse or tripping the breaker.

## Single-Point Ground

All chassis ground terminals in a station should be connected to a **single-point ground** — one central grounding point to which all equipment grounds connect with short, direct conductors. This prevents ground loops, which can cause hum and RF feedback.

## Bonding Ground Electrodes

All ground electrodes (e.g., ground rod, cold water pipe, electrical service ground) must be **bonded together with heavy conductors**. In the event of a **lightning strike**, this prevents **voltage differences between devices** — if different parts of the station are at different potentials during a strike, equipment is destroyed and personnel are endangered.

## Ground Rod Material

Best material for a ground rod driven into the earth: **copper-clad steel**. Copper provides excellent conductivity and corrosion resistance at the surface; steel provides the mechanical strength to be driven deep without bending.

## RF Ground Problems

A ground wire running 10 metres down to a ground rod may work well at DC and audio frequencies, but at RF it is a different story. A 10-metre wire has significant inductance and may be resonant on some amateur bands — presenting **high impedance** rather than low impedance. Result: RF ground is ineffective, and you may experience **RF burns when touching equipment** during transmission. Fix: use shorter, wider straps; use multiple parallel paths; use an RF counterpoise.

## Fuse Ratings

**Never use a fuse with a higher current rating than specified**. A fuse protects the wiring, not the equipment. An oversized fuse lets fault current flow long enough to cause **permanent damage, including a fire**. The fuse must be rated for the current the wiring can safely carry.

**Voltage rating of a fuse**: A fuse also has a voltage rating that specifies the maximum voltage it can interrupt without arcing across the open fuse element. An arcing fuse provides no protection.

## Green Wire in AC Cord

In a three-wire AC line cord, the **green wire** is the safety ground. It connects to the **chassis** (not to neutral, not to the circuit). This is the path for fault current.

## Discharging Filter Capacitors

Before working on a power supply that has been taken offline:
1. Open the cabinet
2. **Discharge the filter capacitors** — the first thing to do. Capacitors can hold lethal voltages for minutes or hours after power is removed.
3. Use an **insulated shorting stick with an inline resistor** to safely discharge them (limits the discharge current to prevent damaging the capacitor or creating a violent arc).

## Exam-Critical Facts

- Best shock protection: ground **all station equipment**
- All chassis grounds connect to: **station's single-point ground**
- Bonding ground electrodes: prevents **voltage differences between devices** during lightning
- Best ground rod material: **copper-clad steel**
- RF burn touching HF transceiver: likely cause is **high impedance on operating frequency** in the ground wire
- Never use fuse with **higher current rating than specified** — risk of fire
- Fuse voltage rating: specifies voltage that can be **interrupted without arcing**
- Green wire in AC cord: connects to **chassis**
- First thing after opening power supply cabinet: **discharge the filter capacitors**
- Safe discharge method: **insulated shorting stick with inline resistor**
- AC line ground purpose: ensures chassis **does not become energized** if fault occurs
